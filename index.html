The Global Trigger

Visualisation 1: The Pulse of the Strait

The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical maritime oil chokepoint. Even though Australia feels geographically isolated from Middle Eastern conflicts, our modern economy relies entirely on the uninterrupted flow of global shipping through this narrow passage (Paul, 2025).

What to Observe:

This area chart tracks weekly cargo ship and oil tanker volume. Notice the sharp, sudden drop-off in vessel transits starting at the end of February. As a regional blockade ground tanker activity to a near-halt, global supplies constricted instantly.

Visualisation 2: The Shockwave Hits Home

Because oil is a globally traded commodity, a supply disruption anywhere on earth triggers an immediate financial reaction everywhere else. This slope graph demonstrates how the shipping bottleneck in the Middle East directly impacted everyday Australians at the bowser.

What to Observe:

Comparing retail fuel prices across Australia immediately before the 2026 crisis to the weeks following demonstrates a universal, aggressive upward trajectory. From Sydney to Perth, prices spiked sharply to historic highs. Use the checkboxes below to see which cities experienced the most extreme price surges.

Our Fragile Lifelines

Visualisation 3: Mapping the Supply Gap

Australia's primary source of oil is from Asian refining hubs such as Singapore, Malaysia, South Korea rather than the Middle East. This highlights a structural vulnerability. Australia does not directly import the majority of its crude, we rely on Asian nations that buy raw crude through the Strait of Hormuz, refine it, and sell it to us. If their raw supply drops, our refined supply vanishes as a consequence, albiet with a delay.

Visualisation 4: State Dependencies

Fuel use per capita (per 100,000 people in this case) is highly uneven across Australian states and territories, driving asymmetric exposure to macroeconomic shocks of an oil supply crisis and an increase in cost.

State Consumption: Western Australia and the Northern Territory exhibit the heaviest fuel dependency per 100,000 residents due to mining, farming, and massive transit spans. Conversely, highly urbanized states like New South Wales, Victoria, and Tasmania have significantly lower per-capita usage, showing that Australia's economic vulnerability to oil shocks is highly unequal across state borders.

Visualisation 5: The Fuel Blueprint (National Consumption Mix)

Not all fuels are created equal; saving our economy requires knowing exactly where our energy pressure points are concentrated via national petroleum product sales.

What to Observe:

Diesel stands out as the absolute heavyweight of the Australian economy, commanding a footprint over double the size of the next largest category, Automotive Petrol. This is due to Australia's extreme reliance on heavy freight (trucking), farming equipment, and interstate logistics/transportation.

Within the Automotive Petrol category, regular <95 octane forms the largest baseline, with all other automative fuels combined still being less than <95 octane fuels, showing that higher quality automotive fuels are not relief upon as much.

Aviation Kerosene use is split between international and domestic use, with ~42% of Jet A-1 (commercial aviation) fuel being used for domestic aviation, and ~58% of Jet A-1 being used for international flights.

The Domestic Countdown

With imports restricted, Australia must survive on its domestic sovereign reserves. These two visualizations simulate inventory lifespans and reveal seasonal trends critical to policy planning.

Visualisation 6: Scarcity Simulations & Stock Countdown

Simulation Analysis: Under normal buying factors, our safety net is razor-thin: we hold just 35 days of Diesel, 29 days of Aviation Kerosene, and 42 days of Automotive Gasoline. Panic buying behavior rapidly shrinks these reserves, while shifting to Aggressive Fuel Rationing demonstrates the power of emergency conservation measures.

Policy Planning Dashboard

Adjusting behavior matrices dynamically illustrates how consumer panic cuts operational reserve margins, making rapid administrative intervention critical to preserving supply continuity.

Visualisation 7: Seasonal Volatility & Demand Rhythms

The monthly area and small-multiple charts reveal the predictable peaks and troughs of fuel demand across a calendar year, serving as crucial baseline data for demand-side planning.

Seasonal Observation: Total fuel consumption bottoms out in February, while hitting its maximum during the October harvesting and end-of-year December holiday rushes. Diesel peaks aggressively in October to power regional agricultural harvesting (Gjerek et al., 2019). Aviation fuels show clear vacation spikes, whereas regular unleaded fuel remains remarkably stable year-round compared to the sharp December surges found in premium fuel blends, presumably as a result of increased holiday travel domestically with exotic and high performance road cars during the summer.

The Path to Resilience

Visualisation 8: The Energy Barter

While Australia is starved for liquid fuel, we are an absolute superpower in gaseous energy, specifically Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG). This interactive diverging bar chart tracks the volume of our refined oil imports against our massive LNG exports to individual trading partners.

There is an undeniable strategic overlap: our top LNG customers—including Japan, South Korea, China, Malaysia, Singapore, and Taiwan—are the exact same nations that provide us with our refined petroleum products. This indicates that Australia holds a massive geopolitical bargaining chip.

Visualisation 9: The Digital Horizon

To fund a transition toward long-term fuel independence and secure strategic reserves, policymakers have floated a controversial mechanism: implementing a 25% Gas Export Tax. This time-series line graph tracks Google search interest within Australia.

Prior to February 2026, terms like "Australian Gas Export Tax", "Gas Export Tax", and "PRRT" registered near-zero public interest. Following the supply squeeze, search frequencies accelerated rapidly through March, eventually peaking in late April and early May.

Visualisation 10: The Unified Verdict

This donut chart breaks down the definitive public polling response to the proposed 25% export levy using the latest consolidated demographic data.

61% Total National Support
5% Total Disagreement

Overwhelming consensus: The data reveals an overwhelming consensus with a resounding 61% of Australians in support of an export tax (26% strongly agreeing and 35% agreeing). What makes this insight remarkable is its universal uniformity—high support is sustained across every age group, state, and political affiliation. Disagreement is practically non-existent, totaling just 5% (4% disagreeing, 1% strongly disagreeing).

Will this unprecedented unity translate into swift legislative action, or will political inertia and industry lobbying dilute the public mandate for change?
